Tiger Anatomy

Skeletal System

Tigers possess a robust skeletal structure consisting of approximately 432 bones. The spine is highly flexible, comprising 7 cervical vertebrae in the neck, 13 thoracic vertebrae in the chest region, 7 lumbar vertebrae in the lower back, and 3 sacral vertebrae fused to form the pelvis. This vertebral column supports the body’s weight and enables powerful movements such as leaping and pouncing.

The skull is large and rounded, housing powerful jaws equipped with long canines (up to 3 inches) for gripping and killing prey. The lower jaw is almost straight, allowing for efficient biting. The clavicle is small, facilitating a wide range of limb movements.

The limbs are muscular and adapted for predation. The hind legs are longer than the front legs, providing leverage for jumping distances up to 20 feet. Each front paw has five fingers with retractable claws, while the hind paws have four fingers. The dewclaw on the front paws does not touch the ground during walking.

The entire skeleton accounts for about 20% of the tiger’s total body weight.

Muscular System

Tigers have over 600 muscles that contribute to their strength, agility, and predatory capabilities. The musculature is highly developed, particularly in the limbs and torso.

Forelimb Musculature

Dissections reveal conserved muscular anatomy across felids, with adaptations for terrestrial locomotion and large prey capture:

  • Shoulder muscles: Relatively smaller compared to other felids, with a prominent m. supraspinatus for shoulder flexion.
  • Arm muscles: Expanded origin for m. triceps brachii caput longum, and relatively large m. triceps brachii caput laterale for elbow extension.
  • Forearm muscles: Large mm. brachioradialis, abductor digiti I longus, and abductor digiti V for wrist and digit movement.
  • Intrinsic muscles: Well-developed for digit flexion and extension.

Muscles of the cranial compartment of the brachium and antebrachium are not as well-developed as in arboreal species, while pronators and supinators are larger.

Hindlimb Musculature

The hindlimbs show differences from other large cats:

  • Presence of m. vastus lateralis with two heads of origin.
  • Two muscles associated with abduction of the fifth digit.
  • Larger muscles involved in ankle inversion and overall hindlimb size compared to forelimbs in homologous joints.

Hindlimb muscles are generally larger than forelimb muscles at corresponding joints, except for digital flexors and extensors.

General Musculature

The muscles allow for behaviors such as crouching, growling (involving jaw muscles), and tail movement for balance during sharp turns.

Organ Systems

Cardiovascular System

The heart is similar in structure to that of domestic carnivores. The left atrium receives four pulmonary veins. The right ventricle has prominent musculus papillaris magnus and m. papillaris subarterial of similar size. The left ventricle features a larger musculus papillaris subauricularis.

Trabeculae carneae are conspicuous throughout the ventricles, and the interventricular septum is muscular. The heart has a more elongated shape compared to the globular heart of dogs.

Coronary arteries originate from the aortic sinuses, with the left coronary artery dividing into circumflex and descending branches.

Digestive System

Tigers have a carnivorous digestive system adapted for meat consumption. The tongue is rough due to numerous papillae, aiding in stripping flesh from bones.

Sensory Organs

  • Eyes: Large, with circular pupils and yellow irises. Possess a tapetum lucidum for excellent night vision and binocular vision for depth perception.
  • Ears: Sharp hearing for detecting prey.
  • Nose: Keen sense of smell for tracking.
  • Other senses: Well-developed touch and taste.

Other Systems

  • Respiratory System: Efficient for high oxygen demand during hunts.
  • Reproductive System: Adapted for seasonal breeding.
  • Nervous System: Highly developed for coordination and reflexes.

This anatomy enables tigers to be apex predators, combining strength, speed, and stealth.
