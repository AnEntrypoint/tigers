# Tiger Fossils

The fossil record of tigers (Panthera tigris) provides insights into their evolutionary history, dating back to the Pliocene epoch.

## Overview

Tigers originated in Asia and have diversified across the continent. The earliest known pantherine fossils are from the Late Miocene to Early Pliocene, with the oldest being Panthera blytheae from the Tibetan Himalaya, estimated at 4.1-5.95 million years ago (Ma).

## Key Fossil Species and Findings

- **Panthera palaeosinensis**: Described from early Pleistocene northern China (approximately 2.5-1.9 Ma). Initially thought to be a tiger ancestor, it is now considered basal to the Panthera genus. Skull measurements indicate a jaguar-like build.

- **Panthera zdanskyi**: A new species from Longdan, Gansu Province, China, dated to 2.55-2.16 Ma. The skull is surprisingly similar to modern tigers, suggesting early anatomical specialization.

- **Panthera blytheae**: Discovered in the Zanda Basin of the Tibetan Himalaya. Represents the oldest known pantherine, with a partial cranium showing features shared with living big cats.

- **Prehistoric subspecies fossils**: Includes Panthera tigris trinilensis and P. t. soloensis from Java and Sumatra; P. t. acutidens from China. These are middle to late Pleistocene in age.

- **Other Pleistocene fossils**: Found in Borneo, Palawan (Philippines), and possibly Japan. Late Pleistocene and early Holocene tiger fossils indicate widespread distribution.

## Ancient DNA and Genetic Studies

Recent studies have analyzed ancient DNA from specimens, including a 10,600-year-old Russian Far East specimen. Genetic admixture occurred during tiger evolution, with Southwest China serving as a Late Pleistocene refugium for a relic basal lineage. Modern tiger populations originated from an Indochina refugium after the Last Glacial Maximum.

## Paleontological Significance

The fossil record shows tigers evolved in Asia, with early diversification. Fossils help calibrate molecular phylogenies, confirming the Asian origin of big cats. Ongoing discoveries may fill gaps in the record.
