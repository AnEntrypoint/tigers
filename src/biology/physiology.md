# Physiology

Tigers (Panthera tigris) are apex predators with highly adapted physiological systems supporting their carnivorous lifestyle, solitary hunting, and survival in diverse habitats. This document covers key bodily functions including digestion, circulation, respiration, nervous system, endocrine system, and more.

## Digestive System

Tigers are obligate carnivores with an efficient digestive system optimized for processing large amounts of meat and even bones. The digestive process begins in the mouth with 30 sharp teeth, including long canines (up to 3 inches) for killing and tearing prey. The rough tongue, covered in papillae, helps strip meat from bones.

Food enters the short esophagus and reaches the stomach, which secretes powerful acids capable of breaking down bones and tough tissues. The small intestine absorbs nutrients, while the large intestine processes waste. Tigers can consume up to 40 kg of meat in one sitting and may fast for days, relying on their ability to store energy.

Key adaptations: High protein and fat digestion, minimal fermentation (unlike herbivores), and rapid nutrient extraction to fuel high-energy activities.

## Circulatory System

The circulatory system delivers oxygen and nutrients throughout the body via a four-chambered heart, arteries, veins, and capillaries. Tigers have robust cardiovascular systems to support bursts of speed and strength.

Blood composition includes red blood cells for oxygen transport, white blood cells for immunity, and platelets for clotting. Cardiac biomarkers like cTnI (cardiac troponin I), NT-proBNP (N-terminal pro-brain natriuretic peptide), and CK-MB (creatine kinase-MB) indicate myocardial health and hemodynamic status.

Reference values from conscious tigers: BUN 15 ± 2 mg/dL, creatinine 1.2 ± 0.1 mg/dL, sodium 151 ± 2 mEq/L, potassium 4.6 ± 0.4 mEq/L, chloride levels balanced for electrolyte homeostasis.

The system integrates with renal function for osmotic regulation and nitrogen turnover.

## Respiratory System

As mammals, tigers use lungs for gas exchange. The respiratory system includes nostrils, trachea, bronchi, and alveoli where oxygen is absorbed and carbon dioxide expelled.

Tigers have efficient lungs adapted for endurance and recovery after exertion. During rest or ambush, respiration is slow; during chases, it ramps up to meet oxygen demands. The diaphragm and intercostal muscles facilitate breathing.

Adaptations: High lung capacity relative to body size, supporting anaerobic bursts during hunts.

## Nervous System

The nervous system coordinates sensory input, motor responses, and cognitive functions. It consists of the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) and peripheral nervous system (nerves to muscles and organs).

The brain is large for a carnivore, enabling complex hunting strategies, territory defense, and social cues. Sensory processing is acute: excellent night vision (6 times better than humans due to tapetum lucidum), keen hearing (up to 60 kHz), and sensitive whiskers for touch and vibration detection.

Autonomic functions control involuntary processes like heart rate and digestion.

## Endocrine System

Hormones regulate metabolism, growth, reproduction, and stress responses. Key glands include the pituitary, thyroid, adrenal, and gonads.

- Pituitary: Releases hormones for growth and reproduction.
- Thyroid: Controls metabolism.
- Adrenal: Produces cortisol for stress (e.g., during hunts or threats).
- Gonads: Testosterone in males for aggression and mating; estrogen/progesterone in females for reproduction.

Endocrine imbalances can affect behavior and health, such as reduced fertility in captivity.

## Muscular and Skeletal System

Tigers have 600 muscles and 432 bones forming a flexible, powerful frame. The backbone provides central support, with a broad head, thick neck, and long tail for balance.

Muscles are highly developed for leaping, pouncing, and dragging prey. Claws are retractable, and jaws exert immense force. Adaptations include small clavicles for greater reach and hind legs longer than front for propulsion.

## Sensory Systems

- **Vision**: Binocular, with night vision enhanced by tapetum lucidum. Limited color vision but excellent motion detection.
- **Hearing**: Sensitive ears rotate 180 degrees to locate sounds up to 300-500 Hz.
- **Smell**: Modest olfactory cells for detecting scents from other tigers or prey.
- **Touch**: Whiskers (vibrissae) detect air pressure changes; sensitive skin.
- **Taste**: Few taste buds, adapted for meat detection.

## Urinary and Excretory System

Kidneys filter waste, producing urea. Tigers conserve water in arid habitats, excreting concentrated urine. The urinary system maintains electrolyte balance, crucial for dehydration-prone species.

Reference values: Uric acid 4.5 ± 0.6 mg/dL, SDMA 8.0 ± 0.5 μg/dL for renal function assessment.

## Reproductive System

Covered in separate files (mating.md, gestation.md, cubs.md), but physiologically: Males have internal testes; females have ovaries and uterus. Hormonal cycles support seasonal mating.

## Immune System

Robust immunity defends against pathogens. White blood cells and antibodies combat infections common in wild environments.

## Integumentary System

Fur and skin provide camouflage, insulation, and protection. Stripes aid in hunting; white belly for thermoregulation.

Overall, tiger physiology is finely tuned for predation, with high efficiency in energy use and adaptation to environmental stresses.
